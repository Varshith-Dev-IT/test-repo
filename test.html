Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR)

What Makes GPR Unique? Unlike drilling or excavation, GPR provides a non-destructive window into the Earth. From concrete bridges to ancient ruins, from hidden pipelines to aquifers, GPR reveals what lies beneath — quickly, safely, and in high resolution.

Our Edge

Our GPR systems feature dual-antenna power with 250 MHz for depth penetration and 500 MHz for high-resolution detail. We provide 3D mapping and visualization to transform profiles into volumetric models. Our smart processing includes advanced filters, imaging, and AI-powered insights. We take an integrated approach, combining GPR with seismic, electrical, and magnetic methods for a complete picture. All work is conducted by experienced geophysicists and geologists.

Culvert Health Monitoring

Problem Statement: Reinforced concrete culverts are critical components in transportation and drainage infrastructure. Over time, they are subjected to traffic loading, environmental exposure, moisture ingress, chloride penetration, and material fatigue, which may lead to deterioration, corrosion of reinforcement, void formation, and loss of serviceability.

Overview: Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR) was deployed for a non-destructive evaluation (NDE) of a reinforced concrete culvert to assess its structural integrity and support preventive maintenance planning. The survey covered 144.5 m² with a high-density 10 cm grid spacing.

Key Findings: Rebar Mesh Mapping, Interface Characterization, Material Degradation Mapping, Corrosion Potential Zones, and Structural Elements were precisely localized.

Indoor GPR Survey for Utility Detection

Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR) was deployed for non-destructive evaluation to detect utilities and map subsurface conditions indoors. The survey enables precise utility location without disruption to operations.

Detection and Mapping of Manholes

Underground manholes and inspection chambers are vital for drainage, utility access, and maintenance. However, their depth, extent, and connectivity are often poorly documented. A GPR survey was conducted to map depth, extent, internal structure, and connecting utilities non-destructively.

Hidden Joints for Roads

A Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR) survey was conducted over a 150 m² road section to assess subsurface layers and detect hidden joints. A 500 MHz ultra-wideband antenna was used to capture both shallow and moderately deep structural features. The survey identified hidden transverse and longitudinal joints, soil subsidence zones, and cracking indicators.

Utilities Beneath Rebar Mesh

Locating utilities beneath reinforced concrete structures is challenging. The dense rebar mesh produces strong reflections that mask deeper anomalies. A 250 MHz ultra-wideband (UWB) antenna was used to detect utilities beneath a reinforced concrete slab, successfully resolving interference caused by rebar signatures while imaging deeper subsurface utilities.

Underground Pipe Network Detection

Efficient irrigation systems rely on well-laid underground pipelines, which are often difficult to detect once covered by soil and vegetation. A Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR) survey was conducted to identify and map the subsurface pipe network supporting the sprinkler system. A 500 MHz ultra-wideband antenna was used to capture shallow subsurface features with high clarity, successfully detecting continuous linear hyperbolic signatures corresponding to small-diameter pipes.
